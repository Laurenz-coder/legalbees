Overview

The EU Whistleblower Directive was adopted to strengthen legal protections for whistleblowers across the European Union. It is designed to protect those who report breaches of EU law in a work-related context from retaliation. The directive covers a wide range of areas, including public procurement, financial services, money laundering, product and transport safety, nuclear safety, public health, consumer and data protection, and more.

Implementation

The directive was adopted by the European Parliament in October 2019, with EU member states given two years to implement it into their national laws. This means that by December 2021, all EU member states should have implemented the directive.

Risk Evaluation

The directive requires organizations of a certain size to establish secure reporting channels for whistleblowers. It promotes a three-tier reporting system where whistleblowers are encouraged to report internally first, then to competent authorities, and finally to the public if the response is inadequate.

Penalties

The directive requires that penalties be imposed for retaliation against whistleblowers, for breaching the confidentiality of whistleblowers, and for making malicious or abusive whistleblower reports.

Updates

By the end of 2021, companies and public bodies with 250 or more employees must implement an internal reporting system. From 2023, this requirement extends to organizations with 50 or more employees.
